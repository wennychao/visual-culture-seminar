Do you suffer from “mobile phone addiction"?

Wenny chao | Dec. 5th. 2023 | Visual Seminar culture

“Jobs didn’t just sell a phone; he sold an experience and a promise of innovation, seamlessly weaving together technology and design. His presentation was not only a showcase of a groundbreaking device but also a lesson in simplicity and elegance, demonstrating how powerful a minimalist approach can be in communication and design. This moment serves as a profound inspiration for aspiring designers, highlighting the importance of user-centric design and the impact of effective storytelling.” By Cristina Miquel Garrido

But do you know that the top executives of social networking sites don’t even use their own products?Because they know that behind the constant swiping of mobile phones is a game that manipulates human weaknesses. These have been brought to my attention that whether the great user-centered design is actually causing your phone addiction.

“iPhone is a game changer at the moment. It is evolutionary and effects how people live from all over the world. Before having touch screen, phone on market worked based on keyboard and small screen. I remembered the year when apple release their product, my dad got one. And I was so curious about this product and try to figure out how to use them like an explorer. The interface is so cool and fluent. I was shocked at the moment because we can play fruit ninja with multiple fingers on screen at the same time.” - by Sharon Liang

Since the advent of smart phones, almost all users have become "addicted" unknowingly. They scroll non-stop as soon as they pick up their phones, which even affects their work, rest, and physical and mental health. Media analysis pointed out that people’s addiction to mobile phone swiping is no accident.

“The article "Invisible Details of Interaction Design" by Rauno Freiberg touched upon an interesting subject of how digital interaction design inherits traits from the humanistic world. One example the article shared that stood out was swiping horizontally to navigate between pages where the characteristics of this action derived from thousands of years of human intuition. All of these small details and actions that interaction designers would process are seemingly taken from natural human instincts that exist way before the digital era.” - by Vicky Chen

It turns out that all this comes from the design of device and APP designers based on "User Experience" and neuropsychology. When your post gets a like or a big heart, It will stimulate your brain to release dopamine, bringing you a feeling of pleasure. And that’s not all. People are now so reliant on their smartphones that the devices are 'becoming our homes', experts warn.

“Last year, I had my phone stolen from me. I was fortunate enough to be able to buy a phone the next day, but the experience of moving through the world with no Google maps or being able to text someone in case I got lost was such a jarring feeling. I think the tech industry is eager to try and create the next revolutionary product- AI, virtual reality, augmented reality. I wonder what will actually stick and truly change the way we exist in this world.” - by Kirsten Geiger
“Apple has become more than just a product; it's akin to a belief system that numerous individuals cannot imagine their lives without. As an increasing number of people depend on iPhones for various aspects of their lives, such as business, social interactions, and personal management, there's a genuine concern about how individuals would cope if the internet were suddenly unavailable. The reliance on Apple products for essential aspects of daily living raises the question of human adaptability in the absence of such technology.”- by Emily Hsieh

As early as 2011, Apple founder Steve Jobs admitted frankly in an interview with the New York Times that “his children cannot freely use technology products at home." At that time, Jobs' youngest daughter was only 13 years old, and has never used the iPad launched by Apple.

Now, not only Jobs, but also many employees at the Silicon Valley headquarters of Google, Apple, Yahoo and other major companies are competing to send their children to the Waldorf School of Peninsula, a private school that emphasizes contact with nature, handicraft creation and play, allowing children to stay away from technology products for a while. In fact, 75% of students’ parents at Waldorf school are senior executives in the technology industry.

Living in the digital age without the Internet will indeed cause inconvenience to study and work, but compared with the worries and criticisms of these "tech rich" who have become rich through technology, perhaps learning how to control technology autonomously, instead of learning how to use technology is a more important lessons that everyone should work on.
